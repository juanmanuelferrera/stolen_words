# Why Do Some Devotees Oppose *Stolen Words* — Even Though They Agree the Revision Was Wrong?

---

**Their concern:**

The book presents the original as "heart-centered mysticism" and the revision as "mind-centered systematization" — and then says both paths are valid. For a devotee committed to Prabhupada's original, this feels like a betrayal: "You just proved our version is the authentic one, and now you're saying the counterfeit is equally valid?"

They fear that a non-devotee reads the book, sees "both are valid paths," and picks the revision — or worse, concludes that it doesn't matter which version you read, which undermines the entire campaign to restore the original.

---

**Is that fear justified?**

**For a non-devotee:** No. A non-devotee who reads this book will overwhelmingly side with the original. The entire weight of evidence — the neuroscience, the smoking guns, the cover-up, the whistleblower — points one direction. The "both are valid" conclusion reads as generosity, not equivalence. No reasonable reader finishes this book thinking "the revision was fine." They finish thinking "that was dishonest, and the original deserves restoration." The balanced conclusion actually makes the evidence *more* credible because the author isn't foaming at the mouth.

**For a devotee:** The concern has some legitimacy, but it misreads the strategy. A devotee already knows the original is superior — they don't need the book to tell them that. What they need is a tool that convinces *outsiders* — academics, journalists, new seekers, the curious public. And a book that concludes "burn the revision" would never reach those audiences. It would be dismissed as sectarian propaganda before page five. The "both are valid" framing is what gets the book into the hands of people who would never touch an ISKCON internal polemic.

---

**The theological contradiction of the objection:**

If the revision is genuinely superior, it should survive open comparison. If devotees freely choose the revision after reading both side by side, that vindicates the editors. The fact that defenders resist comparison rather than welcome it suggests they already know what the result would be.

There's a parallel in the Bhagavatam itself — SB 1.1.1 says the text rejects *kaitava dharma* (cheating religion). The book's argument is precisely that: replacing an author's words without disclosure is *kaitava*, cheating. Asking for transparency is not an attack on the tradition; it's an application of the tradition's own standard.

---

**The book as mirror, not weapon:**

These devotees want the book to be a *weapon*. The author designed it to be a *mirror*. A weapon destroys the enemy. A mirror makes the enemy see themselves.

The institution can fight a weapon — they've been doing it for forty years, dismissing critics as faithless, envious, materialistic. But they cannot fight a mirror that simply says: "Here are both versions. You choose."

The devotees opposing the book are making the same mistake as the institution — they want to control what people conclude instead of trusting them with evidence. Ironically, that's the exact paternalism the book indicts: "We know what's best for you, so we'll decide for you."

---

**The final irony:**

Prabhupada distributed his books on the street to *anyone* — hippies, professors, drunks — and trusted that the truth would speak for itself. This book does the same thing. The discomfort some devotees feel is the discomfort of trusting the reader.

The devotees calling it "dangerous" are actually the book's best marketing. They confirm that the question "why weren't readers allowed to choose?" has no comfortable answer.
