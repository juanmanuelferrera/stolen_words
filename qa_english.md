# STOLEN WORDS — Q&A Script

---

**What is the book Stolen Words about?**

In 1972, Prabhupada gave the world his Bhagavad-gita As It Is. After his death in 1977, a group of editors decided to "improve" his work. They made thousands of changes — not typo corrections, but alterations that transformed the meaning of the text. And they published it under the same title, as if it were the same book. Stolen Words documents exactly what they changed, how they did it, and what effect it has on the reader.

---

**What kind of changes did they find?**

There are three main patterns. First, they changed how Krishna is presented: twenty-two times they replaced intimate, devotional language with formal, hierarchical titles. Second, they turned simple English into technical terminology — where Prabhupada wrote for anyone, the revision demands philosophical credentials. And third, the most subtle: where the original described eternal, unconditional relationships with God, the revision inserted conditions. Grace became effort. Gift became attainment. Love became laboratory.

---

**And why does that matter? It's the same message in the end, isn't it?**

No. And that's precisely the trap. Readers of the original develop intimate devotional consciousness based on grace. Readers of the revision develop methodical religious practice based on knowledge. These are two completely different spiritual trajectories. The book demonstrates this with neuroscience and psycholinguistic analysis — it's not an opinion, it's verifiable evidence.

---

**So what's the conclusion? That we should burn the revised version?**

No. And that's where the book surprises you. The protagonist, after investigating all of this thoroughly, decides to keep both editions. The proposal isn't to censor or destroy anything. It's much simpler and far more devastating: publish both versions, side by side, and let the reader choose consciously.

---

**Why is that more devastating than simply attacking the revision?**

Because it shifts the question. The problem was never that a revision existed — any text can have multiple editions. The problem is that they pulled the original from circulation. They told distributors it had "errors" and should be destroyed. They substituted the book without informing anyone. If the revision were legitimate, why did they need to hide the original?

---

**So the real question isn't "which one is better?"...**

Exactly. The real question is: why weren't you allowed to decide for yourself? You had the right to know that two versions existed with distinct spiritual orientations. You had the right to compare and choose. That right was taken from you. That's why the book is called Stolen Words — they didn't just steal Prabhupada's words, they stole your ability to consciously choose your spiritual path.

---

**Who is this book for?**

For anyone who cares about textual authenticity. You don't need to be a devotee of Krishna. If you care about sacred books not being rewritten after the author's death, if you care about editorial integrity, if you care about how language shapes human consciousness — this concerns you. What happened to the Bhagavad-gita could have happened to the Bible, the Quran, or the Tao Te Ching. It simply happened to the Gita. And because the original survived, we can see exactly how the mechanism works.
