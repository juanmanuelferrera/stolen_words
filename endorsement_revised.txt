STOLEN WORDS: An Endorsement

In 1972, A. C. Bhaktivedanta Swami Prabhupāda gave the world his Bhagavad-gītā As It Is—a devotional translation that introduced millions to Krishna consciousness. After his passing in 1977, his disciples published what they called a "revised and enlarged" edition. But how does one revise a book that claimed to present things "as they are"?

STOLEN WORDS is a literary investigation that reads like a detective story, yet documents something far more consequential: the systematic alteration of a sacred text and the transformation of consciousness that followed.

Through compelling narrative and meticulous textual analysis, this book reveals what happens when an author's work is thoroughly rewritten after his death and published under the same title—not as a new edition with changes clearly marked, but as a wholesale substitution presented as the original.

Using fictional characters to illustrate verifiable patterns, the author guides us through a startling discovery: the 1972 and 1983 editions don't merely differ in wording—they create fundamentally different spiritual trajectories. Readers of the original develop intimate devotional consciousness through grace-dependent transformation. Readers of the revision develop methodical religious practices through knowledge-based progression.

The evidence is documented, verifiable, and disturbing. Thousands of changes. Two distinct theological paths. Millions of readers unaware they're not reading what the original author wrote.

This is not a sectarian dispute or partisan attack. It's a forensic examination of textual integrity, authorial intent, and how language shapes human consciousness. The narrative unfolds with the precision of scholarship and the engagement of literary fiction, allowing readers to witness the discovery process firsthand.

Whether you care about the Bhagavad-gītā specifically or the broader questions of textual authenticity, authorial integrity, and institutional responsibility for preserving sacred texts—this investigation concerns you.

The book makes a singular, essential argument: readers deserve to know what they're reading. They deserve informed consent. They deserve to choose their spiritual path with accurate understanding, not unconscious selection shaped by posthumous editorial decisions.

STOLEN WORDS doesn't tell you which path to walk. It simply illuminates that there are two distinct paths wearing the same name, and reveals how one replaced the other while claiming to be it.

The proof is clear. The implications cannot be ignored. How you respond is your choice alone.

But first, you must know.
