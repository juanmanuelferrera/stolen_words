STOLEN WORDS: An Endorsement

What happens when a sacred text is rewritten after its author's death—and nobody tells the readers?

In 1972, A. C. Bhaktivedanta Swami Prabhupāda published his Bhagavad-gītā As It Is, a devotional translation that would introduce millions to Krishna consciousness. For five years until his passing in 1977, he personally guarded every word, insisting his books remain exactly as he wrote them. "Do not change even one word," he instructed his disciples.

Six years after his death, a "revised and enlarged" edition appeared. The changes were extensive—thousands of alterations throughout. Yet readers were never informed. The revised edition simply replaced the original on bookstore shelves, bearing the same title, the same author's name, as if nothing had changed.

STOLEN WORDS documents what was lost in that substitution.

This is not mere academic hairsplitting about editorial choices. Through meticulous analysis presented in compelling narrative form, this book demonstrates that the two editions don't just differ in wording—they offer fundamentally different spiritual paths.

The original edition emphasized grace, surrender, and devotional intimacy with the divine. The revised edition shifted toward knowledge, ritual practice, and methodical spiritual progression. Same title. Same author attributed. Completely different consciousness being cultivated in readers.

Prabhupāda had given explicit, recorded instructions that his books were not to be changed. His copyrights were held in a trust he established for their protection. Yet after his passing, decisions were made that contradicted his clearly expressed wishes. Whether you view this as well-intentioned improvement or unauthorized alteration, one fact remains undeniable: readers were never told.

This book makes a case for transparency. Not for one edition over another, but for informed choice.

Using fictional characters to illustrate documented patterns, STOLEN WORDS reads like an investigation—scholarly in its evidence, literary in its presentation. The author avoids inflammatory accusations, letting the textual comparisons speak for themselves. All claims are verifiable through publicly available sources.

The implications extend beyond one tradition. This could have happened to the Bible, the Quran, any sacred text. It happened to the Bhagavad-gītā, and because the original survived, we can observe exactly how posthumous revisions reshape spiritual understanding.

If you care about authorial integrity, textual authenticity, or how institutions handle sacred materials entrusted to their care—this investigation will challenge you.

If you're a sincere spiritual seeker who wants to know whether what you're reading reflects the original author's understanding or someone else's theological preferences—this book provides essential evidence.

Two editions. Two spiritual paths. Millions of readers who deserve to know which one they're actually following.

The documentation is thorough. The patterns are clear. The choice of how to respond belongs to each reader.

But informed consent requires information. STOLEN WORDS provides exactly that.
