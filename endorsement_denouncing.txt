STOLEN WORDS: An Endorsement

A sacred trust was broken. This book documents how.

In 1972, A. C. Bhaktivedanta Swami Prabhupāda published his Bhagavad-gītā As It Is, a devotional translation that would transform millions of lives. He guarded these books zealously, personally reviewing every word, every edition. His instructions were explicit and repeatedly stated: "Do not change my books. Do not change even one word." He established the Bhaktivedanta Book Trust specifically to protect his works for future generations.

In 1977, Prabhupāda passed away, leaving behind his precious gift to humanity—his books, exactly as he wanted them preserved.

Six years later, those entrusted with protecting his legacy published a "revised and enlarged" edition containing thousands of unauthorized changes. The revisions were extensive, systematic, and fundamentally altered the spiritual path the book presented. Yet they were published under Prabhupāda's name, under the same title, with no disclosure to readers that this was not what the author had written.

This was not editing. This was rewriting.

STOLEN WORDS reveals the scope of what occurred. Through careful textual analysis presented in compelling narrative form, this book demonstrates that the changes were not minor corrections but a theological transformation. The original's emphasis on grace and devotional surrender was systematically shifted toward knowledge-based practices and ritual progression.

Prabhupāda's students were given a sacred responsibility: preserve the teachings exactly as their spiritual master presented them. Instead, some chose to "improve" what he had written, convinced they understood better than he did how to present Krishna consciousness to the world.

The presumption is staggering. The consequences are measurable.

Millions of sincere seekers have read the revised edition believing they were receiving Prabhupāda's teachings. They were not. They received a committee's interpretation, posthumously imposed, dressed in the author's clothing. They were denied the choice of which path to follow because they were never told there were two different paths.

This book is not about sectarian disputes. It's about a fundamental breach of trust—by publishers toward their author, by institutions toward their founder, by guardians toward those they were meant to serve.

The evidence is documented and verifiable. The pattern is unmistakable. Prabhupāda said not to change his books. After his death, when he could no longer object, his books were changed extensively. Readers were never informed. That sequence of events speaks for itself.

STOLEN WORDS advocates for something simple: transparency. Readers deserve to know what they're reading. Spiritual seekers deserve authentic access to the teachings they're trying to follow. Authors deserve to have their life's work preserved as they intended, not rewritten according to others' preferences.

Whether you view these revisions as well-intentioned improvements or unauthorized alterations, one question remains: Why weren't readers told? Why the silence? Why the substitution rather than offering both editions and letting people choose?

The original still exists. The evidence of what was changed is available for anyone to verify. This book lays it out clearly, accessibly, compellingly.

What was done cannot be undone. But readers can finally be told the truth. They can be given the choice that was taken from them. They can decide for themselves which spiritual path resonates with their soul—Prabhupāda's original presentation or the posthumous revision.

But first, they must know there is a choice.

STOLEN WORDS ensures they know.

The sacred trust was broken, but the sacred truth can still be told.
